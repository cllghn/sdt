Social Disorganization Theory
A Brief Introduction​

What is it?


  • Social disorganization theory is a macro-level theory that focuses on the distribution of crime rates across areas, not on why any one individual may be more or less likely to engage in criminal acts than another (Kubrin 2010).
  • Social disorganization can be defined as the inability of a community to realize the common values of its residents and maintain effective social controls (Bursik 1988).
  • A key assumption is that communities can be classified on a continuum of disorganization, from high to low. Here, highly disorganized communities experience more crime (Kubrin 2010).

    • Socially organized communities

    • Solidarity: residents want the same things
    • Cohesion: strong bonds among neighbors
    • Integration: recurring social interactions

Origins

Shaw and McKay's Research (1942)

  • Background
  • Maps
  • Data
  • Findings

  • Shaw and McKay applied the ideas of human ecology and the concentric zone model to study the association between urban characteristics and juvenile delinquency.
  • Their study of social disorganization centered around three sets of variables:

    • Physical status: They proposed that areas with high delinquency rates tended to be physically deteriorated, close to heavy industry, and populated with highly transient residents.

    • Economic status: The association between economic status and delinquency.

    • Population status: They relationship between demographics and the rate of delinquency in an area.

  • By hand, they mapped the addresses of juvenile delinquents - those referred to juvenile court, truancy, and recidivism - and compiled them into small census areas and they city zones. Then they examined delinquency in Chicago communities for three periods of time: 1900-1906, 1917-1923, and 1927-1933.

Physical status

They measures the physical status of an area through: (1) population change, (2) vacant and condemned housing, and (3) proximity to industry.


Economic status

They used three variables in their analysis: (1) the number of families receiving social assistance, (2) the median rental price of the area, and (3) the number of homes ownen rather than rented.


Population status

They looked at the demographic variables such as ethnicity, number of foreign-born heads of household, etc.

Cullen et al. (2022) provide a description of the findings of the work:

  • Crime rates were pronounced in the zone of transition and became progressively lower as one moves to the outer zones.
  • Zone in transition was characterized by low socioeconomic status, ethnic heterogeneity, and residential instability.
  • Rates of crime by area remained relatively the same regarless of ethnic turnover. This suggests that characteristics of the area, not the people in them, regulated levels of delinquency.
