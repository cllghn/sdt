BLUF


  • Social disorganization theory (SDT) is a macro-level theory that explains the distribution of crime rates across areas based on structural and cultural factors shaping the nature of social order, not on why any one individual may be more or less likely to engage in criminal acts than another (Kubrin 2010).
  • Social disorganization can be defined as the inability of a community to realize the common values of its residents and maintain effective community-based social controls (Bursik 1988).
  • A key assumption is that communities can be classified on a continuum of disorganization, from high to low. Here, highly disorganized communities experience more crime (Kubrin 2010).

    • Cohesion: strong bonds among neighbors
    • Integration: recurring social interactions

Origins

Shaw and McKay's Research (1942)

  • Background
  • Maps
  • Data
  • Findings

  • Shaw and McKay applied the ideas of human ecology and the concentric zone model to study the association between urban characteristics and juvenile delinquency. On the basis of this research they developed SDT.
  • By hand, they mapped the addresses of juvenile delinquents - those referred to juvenile court, truancy, and recidivism - and compiled them into small census areas and city zones. Then they examined delinquency in Chicago communities for three periods of time: 1900-1906, 1917-1923, and 1927-1933.
  • Their study of social disorganization centered around three sets of variables:

    • Physical status : They proposed that areas with high delinquency rates tended to be physically deteriorated, close to heavy industry, and populated with highly transient residents.

    • Economic status : The association between economic disadvantage and delinquency.

    • Population status: They relationship between demographics and the rate of delinquency in an area.


Physical status

They measures the physical status of an area through: (1) population change, (2) vacant and condemned housing, and (3) proximity to industry.


Economic status

They used three variables in their analysis: (1) the number of families receiving social assistance, (2) the median rental price of the area, and (3) the number of homes owned rather than rented.


Population status

They looked at the demographic variables such as ethnicity, number of foreign-born heads of household, etc.

From Boom to Lean Times

Despite a wave of replication of Shaw and McKay's work (Bordua, 1959; Chilton, 1964; Lander, 1954), the theory lost its appeal 😕 until the 1980s, resurging in the 1990s.

In the 1980's, criminologist began reconsidered the theory because of an interest in the "ecology" of crime.

Bursick (1988) provided criticisms of the theory that served to layout the academic agenda in the next wave of studies:

  • The assumption of stable ecological structures
  • The measurement of social disorganization
  • The measurement of crime and delinquency

Sampson and Groves (1989)

  • Background
  • Model
  • Data
  • Findings
  • Sampson and Groves (1989) expanded on prior work by testing the idea that informal relations and controls would be weakened, leading to high crime, in communities marked by poverty, heterogeneity, residential transiency, and family disruption. Or as they put it: "empirically, the structural dimension of community social disorganization can be measured in terms of prevalence and interdependence of social networks in a community ... and in the span of collective supervision that the community directs toward local problems." (Sampson and Groves 1989, pp. 777).
  • Sampson (1986) argued that crime was high in inner cities because residents had lost the capacity to exercise "informal social control" (Cullen et al. 2022). For example, coming from a neighborhood with a high number of "broken" homes, were adults are not around to supervise the youth.

    • "Fun" Fact 💡

    While a graduate student, Sampson enrolled in a seminar taught by Travis Hirschi who was an advocate of control theory.
  • Prior tests on SDT had only measured the structural "antecedents" or causes of social disorganization and then examined if these factors were related to crime. As they put it "the crux of the problem is that previous macro-level research in crime and delinquency has relied primarily on census data that rarely provide measures for the variables hypothesized to mediate the relationship between community structure and crime." (Sampson and Groves 1989, pp. 775).

Causal model adapted from Sampson and Groves (1989)

  • The data came for this study came from the first British Crime Survey (BCS) , a nationwide survey of England and Wales conducted in 1982.

    • "Fun" Fact 💡

    The latest annual results were published in October 2022.
  • From their theoretical model they constructed variables for:
    • Local friendship networks: Using a question which asked respondents how many of their friends resided in the local community (~15-min walk).
    • Organizational participation: Using a question which asked about the social and leisure activities of respondents for each night of the week. The variable focuses on percentage of residents who participated in meetings of committees and clubs.
    • Supervision of youth peer groups: They computed the percentage of residents who reported disorderly teenage peer groups as "very common" in the neighborhood.
    • Exogenous characteristics from Shaw and McKay model were also derived from the survey.
    • As an indicator of crime, they constructed a total victimization rate to reflect between community variations in serious crimes against people.

  1. Their empirical analysis established that communities characterized by sparse friendship networks, unsupervised teen groups, and low organization participation had disproportionally high rates of crime and delinquency.
  2. The structural factors (poverty, heterogeneity, mobility) predicted their measures of social disorganization and, in turn, those weakly organized communities had higher crime rates.

Systemic Control Model of Social Disorganization

  • Beginning with Sampson and Grove's work, tests of SDT began drawing from the systemic model of community attachment to measure how communities organize for effective control.

    • "Fun" Fact 💡

    The systemic model of community attachment views the community as "a complex system of ... networks and formal and informal ... ties rooted in family life and on-going socialization processes" (Kasarda and Janowitz 1972).
  • Sampson and Groves presumed that weak social control emerged from an absence of ties among neighborhood residents.
  • Robert Bursik and Harold Grasmick (1993) extended this model beyond internal neighborhood ties as sources of organization and control. Their work includes a categories of ties that enhance social control on crime:
    • Private: intimate interpersonal ties (e.g., friendship).
    • Parochial: shared institutional and organizational ties (e.g., attending the same school).
    • Public: connection to agencies outside the neighborhood with resources for controlling crime (e.g., local police).
  • Velez's (2001) examined the value of public ties by studying 60 neighborhoods and uncovering that residents' ties to government and police were associated with lower rates of burglary, assault, and mugging.

Extending Social Disorganization Theory

Collective Efficacy Theory

  • A related alternative to the systemic model of SDT.
  • It includes the willingness of community residents to exercise social control and to trust and help one another on behalf of the common good (Sampson et al. 1997). This concept appears to be the opposite of social disorganization; yet, it differs because:
    • Organization isn't just control, but also mutual trust and support between neighbors.
    • Social disorganization is portrayed as a static factor that one is born into, collective efficacy is dynamic.
  • Collective efficacy is the "process of activating or converting social ties to achieve desired outcomes" (Sampson et al. 1999).

Legal Cynicism and Community Crime

  • A cultural frame at the community level "through which individuals interpret the functioning and viability of the law and its agents, especially law enforcement. When the law is perceived to be just, legitimate, and responsive then individuals are more likely to cooperate and comply with the law." (Kirk and Papachristos 2011).
  • Legal cynicism emerges from:
    • Concentrated disadvantage
    • Previous police-resident encounters
  • Legal cynicism has consequences for neighborhood rates of violence because the police are not relied upon to handle grievances; instead, residents may take matters into their own hands, which may lead to violence.

How can this theory and extensions be useful?

  • Shift the focus from traditional "criminogenic" factors of the individual (e.g., low self-control, anti-social personality) and cast light on urban policies pertaining to infrastructure, poverty reduction, and the buildup of social networks that foment informal controls.
  • Networks, not attributes: Most datasets tend to focus on the non-relational characteristics of offenders. However, attributes (e.g., race, education) do not vary across social contexts (e.g., at school, at work) while ties do. This is why social network analysts focus on the patterns of interaction between actors to predict behavior (Cunningham et al. 2015, pp 10). This idea is reflected in the work of McKay and Shaw; namely, in regards to cultural transmission via links to other delinquents. Also crystalized in the words of Wasserman and Faust (2014) "Network models focusing on individuals view their network structural environment as providing opportunities for or constraints on individual action". Critique on using surveys for measuring social networks...

For the last thirty years, empirical social research has been dominated by the sample survey. But as usually practiced, using random sampling of individuals, the survey is a biological meatgrinder, tearing the individual from his social context and guaranteeing that nobody in the study interact with anyone else in it. It is a little like a biologist putting his experimental animals through a hamburger machine and looking at every hundredth cell through a microscope; anatomy and physiology get lost, structure and function disappear, and one is left with cell biology... If our aim is to understand people's behavior rather than simply record it, we want to know about groups, neighborhoods, organizations, social circles, and communities; about interaction, communication, role expectations, and social control.


Allen Barton, 1968
