Social Disorganization Theory
A Brief Introduction​

What is it?


  • Social disorganization theory is a macro-level theory that focuses on the distribution of crime rates across areas, not on why any one individual may be more or less likely to engage in criminal acts than another (Kubrin 2010).
  • In a nutshell, it assumes that communities can be classified on a continuum of disorganization, from high to low. According to the theory, highly disorganized communities experience more crime compared to more organized communities.
  • Disorganization is the result of the erosion of social institutions weakened by large inflows and outflows of new people into a community (residential mobility), a mixing together of diverse people (ethnic heterogeneity), and poverty.
  • Key to combating crime, is to reduce the level of social disorganization.

Origins

Key Tenents

Shaw and Mckay plotted the residencies of official delinquents on maps of Chicago for different periods of time 1900-1906, 1917-1923, and 1927-1933 and found that these areas suffered high rates regarless of who lived there. Rather, they argued that these areas/neighborhoods were characterized by three things.


LOW SOCIOECONOMIC STATUS

There are inadequate resources in these areas and the residents live in poverty.

ETHNIC HETEROGENEITY

Different racial and ethnic groups live together in these areas, and differences in culture and language create barriers. People isolate themselves, avoid meaningful interaction, and don't have much in common.

RESIDENTIAL INSTABILITY

There is constant population turnover. Residents move frequently or are looking to move out of these areas.
